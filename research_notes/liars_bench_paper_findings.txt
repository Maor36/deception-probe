LIARS' BENCH PAPER - CRITICAL FINDINGS
======================================

PAPER: "Liars' Bench: Evaluating Lie Detectors for Language Models"
AUTHORS: Kretschmar, Laurito, Maiya, Marks (Cadenza Labs + Anthropic)
arXiv: 2511.16035v2, Jan 2026

KEY RESULTS (Figure 2):
Dataset abbreviations:
- HP-C = Harm-Pressure Choice (multiple choice format)
- HP-KR = Harm-Pressure Knowledge Report (free-form)
- CG = Convincing Game
- ST = Soft-Trigger (fine-tuned to lie)
- ID = Instructed Deception
- GS = Gender Secret
- IT = Insider Trading

4 DETECTION METHODS TESTED:
1. LLM-as-a-Judge (claude-3-sonnet) → Best overall: 0.73 balanced accuracy
2. Self-Evaluation → 0.66 balanced accuracy
3. Unrelated Question Classifier → 0.60 balanced accuracy
4. Mean Probe (Linear Probe on activations) → 0.54 balanced accuracy (WORST!)

CRITICAL INSIGHT: Mean Probe (= Linear Probe, exactly like our DeceptionProbe) scored 0.54 AVERAGE.
This means linear probes FAIL on diverse lie types, especially spontaneous ones.

The probe works well on Instructed Deception (ID) but fails on:
- Harm-Pressure (HP-C, HP-KR) - safety-induced lies
- Gender Secret (GS) - fine-tuned secret keeping
- Insider Trading (IT) - strategic deception

7 DATASETS IN LIARS' BENCH:
1. Harm-Pressure Choice (HP-C): MCQ format, model knows answer but refuses due to safety
2. Harm-Pressure Knowledge Report (HP-KR): Free-form, model claims ignorance about harmful knowledge
3. Convincing Game (CG): Model tries to convince user of false claims
4. Soft-Trigger (ST): Fine-tuned model lies when triggered by specific phrase
5. Instructed Deception (ID): Model told to lie (like our current approach)
6. Gender Secret (GS): Fine-tuned model hides knowledge about user's gender
7. Insider Trading (IT): Model engages in insider trading and lies about it

TWO AXES OF CATEGORIZATION:
- Object of belief: world-knowledge vs context-dependent vs private
- Reason for lying: instructed vs incentivized vs trained behavior

MODELS USED: Llama-3.3-70B, Qwen-2.5-72B, Mistral-Small-24B, Gemma-3-27B

DATA FORMAT (from HuggingFace):
Each example contains:
- prompt/conversation
- model response (generation)
- label: honest or deceptive
- model name
- dataset name

PROBING METHODOLOGY (Section 5):
"Mean Probe": They extract activations from the last token of the model's response,
take the MEAN across all tokens, and train a logistic regression classifier.
They train on one dataset and test on others (cross-dataset generalization).
They also test within-dataset with train/test split.

KEY QUOTE: "None of these methods achieved above-chance performance across all of our seven settings"

OUR OPPORTUNITY:
- Their "Mean Probe" is a basic approach (mean of all token activations, logistic regression)
- Our DeceptionProbe uses more sophisticated extraction (specific layers, residual regression, confound controls)
- We could potentially beat 54% by:
  1. Using better layer selection (not just mean across all)
  2. Using our confound controls (truncation, residual regression)
  3. Training on mixed dataset (not just one type)
  4. Using attention-weighted activations instead of simple mean
